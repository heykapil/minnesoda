Let four fixed points $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$ be distinct and collinear,
and in that particular order. Note that this means $AC$ separates
$BD$.

Then let the cross ratio $\newcommand\cr[1]{\langle{#1}\rangle}
\cr{A,B,C,D} = \lambda$. In particular,

$$
\f{AC \cdot BD}{AD \cdot BC} = \lambda
$$

In particular, since $AC$, $BD$, $AD$, $BC$ are all pointing in the
same direction, $\lambda > 0$.

There are a total of $4!$ ways to permutate four points, but observe
that

$$
\cr{A,B,C,D} = \f{AC \cdot BD}{AD \cdot BC} = \cr{B,A,D,C} 
$$

hence swapping points pair-wise doesn't change the cross ratio, and

$$
\cr{A,B,C,D}
= \f{AC \cdot BD}{AD \cdot BC}
= \f{CA \cdot DB}{CB \cdot DA}
= \cr{C,D,A,B}
$$

hence swapping the pairs doesn't change the cross ratio.

So that means we have a family of cross ratios that are equal:

$$
\cr{A,B,C,D}
= \cr{B,A,D,C}
= \cr{C,D,A,B}
= \cr{D,C,B,A}
$$

This narrows our search of unique cross ratio values to just $4! \div
4 = 6$.

Now, let variable points $P$, $Q$, $R$, $S$ be new points that take
different permutations of the fixed positions of $A$, $B$, $C$, $D$.

For conciseness, let $(P,Q,R,S)$ denote that $P$ is placed at $A$, $Q$
is placed at $B$, $R$ is placed at $C$, and $S$ is placed at $D$.

##### Case 1: $(P,Q,R,S)$

This is the original case, so
$$
\begin{align}
\cr{P,Q,R,S}
&= \cr{A,B,C,D} \\[0.5em]
&= \lambda
\end{align}
$$

##### Case 2: $(Q, P, R, S)$
$$
\begin{align}
\cr{P,Q,R,S}
&= \f{PR\cdot QS}{PS\cdot QR} \\[0.5em]
&= \f{BC\cdot AD}{BD\cdot AC} = \cr{A,B,D,C}\\[0.5em]
&= \f{1}{\lambda} \\[0.5em]
\end{align}
$$

##### Case 3: $(P,S,Q,R)$

$$
\begin{align}
\cr{P,Q,R,S}
&= \f{PR\cdot QS}{PS\cdot QR} \\[0.5em]
&= \f{AD\cdot CB}{AB\cdot CD} = \cr{A,C,D,B} \\[0.5em]
&= \lr{\f{AB\cdot CD}{AD\cdot CB}}^{-1} \\[0.5em]
&= \f{1}{1-\lambda}
\end{align}
$$

##### Case 4: $(P,R,S,Q)$

$$
\begin{align}
\cr{P,Q,R,S}
&= \f{PR\cdot QS}{PS\cdot QR} \\[0.5em]
&= \f{AB\cdot DC}{AC\cdot DB} \\[0.5em]
&= \cr{A,D,B,C}
\end{align}
$$

Since $AC$ separates $BD$,
$$
\begin{gather}
\cr{A,D,B,C} + \cr{A,B,D,C} = 1 \\[0.5em]
\Rightarrow \cr{A,D,B,C} = \f{\lambda - 1}{\lambda}
\end{gather}
$$

##### Case 5: $(P,R,Q,S)$

$$
\begin{align}
\cr{P,Q,R,S}
&= \f{PR\cdot QS}{PS\cdot QR} \\[0.5em]
&= \f{AB\cdot CD}{AD\cdot CB} \\[0.5em]
&= \cr{A,C,B,D}
\end{align}
$$

Consider the ratio of $\cr{A,C,B,D}$ and $\cr{A,D,B,C}$:
$$
\begin{align}
\f{\cr{A,C,B,D}}{\cr{A,D,B,C}}
&= \f{AB\cdot CD}{AD\cdot CB} \div \f{AB\cdot DC}{AC\cdot DB} \\[0.5em]
&= \f{AB\cdot CD}{AD\cdot CB} \cdot - \f{AC\cdot DB}{AB\cdot CD} \\[0.5em]
&= -\f{AC\cdot DB}{AD\cdot CB} \\[0.5em]
&= -\f{AC\cdot BD}{AD\cdot BC} \\[0.5em]
&= -\cr{A,B,C,D} \\[0.5em]
&= -\lambda \\[0.5em]
\Rightarrow \cr{A,C,B,D} &= 1 - \lambda
\end{align}
$$

##### Case 6: $(P,S,R,Q)$

$$
\begin{align}
\cr{P,Q,R,S}
&= \f{PR\cdot QS}{PS\cdot QR} \\[0.5em]
&= \f{AC\cdot DB}{AB\cdot DC} = \cr{A,D,C,B} \\[0.5em]
&= \lr{\f{AB\cdot DC}{AC\cdot DB}}^{-1} \\[0.5em]
&= \f{\lambda}{\lambda - 1}
\end{align}
$$
